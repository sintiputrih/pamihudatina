Sauce Walka Career

Walka fell in love with music after he recorded in the Diamond Cuts Studio. His songs are loved by many because it tells his story and illustrates his rough and frightening childhood. Unlike other Houston rappers who talk about drugs and gang wars in their songs, Walka took a different path and it has paid off.

In 2014, he released a mixtape titled In Sauce We Trust, and in 2015, he released his first solo project titled Sorry 4 the Sauce and he also released a second part. While a member of the Twinz, he released 2 Legited 2 Quited which was a mainstream hit. In 2015, he released mixtapes titled Don’t Let The Sauce Fool U and Sauce Theft Auto. Over the years, he has worked, associated, and collaborated with popular names like 21 Savage, Shy Glizzy, Boosie Badazz, Lil Yachty, and lot more.

Walka has also had his own share of controversies. His single No Exception had a cover which demonstrated him being crucified and Drake receiving the dripping sauce. In most of Walka’s songs, he talks about how his mother was addicted and left him and his brother when they were just kids and how his professional wrestling father also deserted them and he had no other option than to embrace the hard life on the streets. During his teenage years, he was a tout and he was also into pimping, but as time went on he got into music and has been dedicated ever since then.

Sauce Walka Legal Issues

Mondane was involved in a gun-related incident in summer of 2009. Court records show that Mondane pleaded guilty to deadly conduct after being charged with shooting and wounding a person during a concert at Texas Southern University, which resulted in him serving community supervision. In 2018, Houston Police told KPRC that Mondane was involved in a criminal street gang called “Mash Mode” in 2009, at the time going by the moniker “A-Walk”.

In 2018, Texas state court documents described The Sauce Factory as a “documented gang” and “known for criminal activity”. In response to the accusations, Mondane stated:

“I’m going to keep rapping because I know I’m not a gang. I know we’re not a gang, I know that I’m a record label, I know that I’m an artist […] I’m not into gang activity or none of that stuff that’s going on, I’m not worried about that, it’s not what’s going on.”
